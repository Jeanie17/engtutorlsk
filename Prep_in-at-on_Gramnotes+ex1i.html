Preposition IN

Used for longer periods of time

IN
  • in the morning
  • in the afternoon
  • in (the) summer
  • in 1980
  • in the 1900s
  • in the 17th century
  • in the Easter holiday
  • in the next century
  • in the future
  • in 10 years time

Rule for IN

Use IN for:

  • Parts of the day (morning, afternoon, evening - but NOT night)
  • Months, seasons, years, decades, centuries
  • Long periods of time (the future, the past, holidays)

Exception: We say "at night" not "in the night"

3

Preposition ON

Used for specific days and dates

ON
  • on Monday
  • on Saturday
  • on June 3rd
  • on 1st October 2013
  • on Christmas day
  • on my birthday
  • on Tuesday evening
  • on time
  • on a winter evening
  • on a warm day

Rule for ON

Use ON for:

  • Days of the week (Monday, Tuesday, etc.)
  • Specific dates (June 3rd, 1st October 2013)
  • Special days (Christmas day, my birthday)
  • Days with parts of the day (Tuesday evening)
  • Specific days described (a winter evening, a warm day)

Note: "On time" means punctual, not early or late

4

Preposition AT

Used for precise times and expressions

AT
  • at 9 am
  • at 8 o'clock
  • at 6 pm
  • at night
  • at noon/midday
  • at Easter
  • at Christmas
  • at dinner time
  • at dinner
  • at that moment

Rule for AT

Use AT for:

  • Clock times (9 am, 8 o'clock, 6 pm)
  • Specific points in the day (night, noon, midnight)
  • Holiday periods (Easter, Christmas) when referring to the period
  • Meal times (breakfast, lunch, dinner)
  • Specific moments (that moment, this time)

Note: We say "at Christmas" for the holiday period but "on Christmas day" for the specific day

5

Rules Summary

When to use each preposition

IN - The Big Picture

Think of IN as the "big container" of time:

in July (the whole month)
in 2023 (the whole year)
in the morning (the whole morning period)

Use for months, years, seasons, centuries

ON - The Specific Day

Think of ON as a specific point on a calendar:

on Monday (a specific day)
on June 15th (a specific date)
on my birthday (a specific day)

Use for days and dates

AT - The Precise Point

Think of AT as a precise point on a clock:

at 3:00 PM (exact time)
at night (the night period)
at Christmas (the holiday period)

Use for clock times and expressions
